A Royal Makeover: Carrots Weren't Always Orange

When you picture a carrot, you see a vibrant orange color. But what if we told you that for centuries, this wasn't the case at all? The iconic orange carrot is not a product of nature's original design, but rather a fascinating piece of human history and selective breeding.

A Rainbow of Roots

Originally, carrots were cultivated in the regions of Persia (modern-day Iran and Afghanistan) as far back as the 10th century. These ancient carrots were not orange at all; they were typically deep purple or sometimes white and yellow. They were grown for their aromatic leaves and seeds rather than their roots.

The Dutch Connection

The transformation to the familiar orange color is largely credited to Dutch growers in the 16th and 17th centuries. The story goes that patriotic Dutch farmers wanted to pay tribute to their ruling family, the House of Orange, led by William of Orange. They began selectively breeding yellow carrot varieties, gradually developing strains with higher concentrations of beta-carotene, the pigment that gives them their orange hue.

This new, vibrant orange carrot was not only a political statement but also a sweeter and less woody variety, which made it far more popular to eat. Its success was so immense that it quickly spread across Europe, and the original purple and yellow varieties became rarities. So, every time you snack on an orange carrot, you're enjoying a tasty piece of Dutch history!
